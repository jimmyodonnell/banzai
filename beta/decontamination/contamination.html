With regard to the Taberlet approach for filtering of negative controls, here's what they did in the De Barba paper (PCR- = negative controls):

"Examination of consensus sequence profiles obtained for any given sample by averaging read counts of the remaining sequences among replicates showed that
PCR- had a total per sample counts considerably smaller than other samples (Fig. 3g-h). We took this as an indication that faecal samples with small total read
counts could reasonably be considered of low DNA quantity for the marker under analysis and therefore more easily exposed to random amplification of the few target molecules present in the template. Following this reasoning, we deleted samples with total mean consensus read count <1000 for all universal and <500 for all ITS markers, which resulted in the removal of all PCR-, and a variable number of faecal samples in all markers data (Table 2)."

So all their negative controls were discarded using this filtering. If we took the same approach, we also have a big discrepancy between the total number of reads in the negative controls (mean=26,700 excluding human sequences) vs. field samples (mean=195,000), but much higher counts in the negative controls. I excluded human sequences because they just dominate the controls and inflate the count total.

Yeah - tricky. One thought I had was to think through why we have controls in the first place: what are we controlling for?

I think we do/did negative controls to ensure we can distinguish sequences in the field from contamination (this is seemingly straightforward, but stay with me). This leaves us with the issue that any species that shows up in the negative controls AND in the field samples might be an artifact of contamination. So, human, rockfish, and a bunch of species that all have a common ancestor within Zoarcoidei (eel-like fish of which several are in Monterey).

By contrast, Taberlet et al. seem to be using the negative controls as controls on the sequencing reaction—essentially, to determine if the sample runs yield reads at a rate greater than a blank. This allows them to identify and remove low-quality samples as likely to be subject to contamination. But that doesn’t really apply in our case, now that I think about it, because we don’t have low-read samples. 

We could use the controls in another way — and we’ve seen this somewhere before — which is as a yardstick for sequencing/annotation at the community level. So, we do a PERMANOVA (adonis), and anything that’s not significantly different from the controls, we throw out.  Anything that is significantly different from the controls, we keep.  That solves much of the problem, but leaves us with some taxa (mostly human, but some others) we think are spurious in the samples. We could then do a secondary sweep, removing taxa that a) are above a critical threshold of abundance in the controls, and/or b) some other reasonable criterion. But does this remove rockfish?
